## Case Overview

The `movingCone` case is a sophisticated example that simulates the aerodynamic effects of a cone moving at 160 m/s through the air. Its primary purpose is to demonstrate the `movingAdaptiveFvMesh` capability in OpenFOAM, which allows the mesh to both deform according to a prescribed motion and adaptively refine based on solution gradients. This is crucial for accurately and efficiently capturing phenomena like bow shocks generated by supersonic objects.

## Physics and Solver

- **Solver**: `blastFoam`. Although often used for detonations, here it is applied to a single-phase, compressible, non-reacting aerodynamic problem.
- **Flow Physics**:
    - **Phase**: The simulation uses a `basic` single-phase model representing air as an `idealGas`.
    - **Turbulence**: The simulation is configured to be `laminar`.
- **Dynamic Mesh**: This is the central feature of the case.
    - **`dynamicFvMesh` Type**: `movingAdaptiveFvMesh`. This combines two powerful features:
        1.  **Mesh Motion**: The `motionSolver` is `velocityComponentLaplacian`, which deforms the mesh based on the prescribed velocity of the moving cone. The motion itself is defined in `0/pointMotionUx`, where a `table` dictates that the cone moves at 160 m/s in the x-direction for a short duration.
        2.  **Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR)**: The `errorEstimator` is `densityGradient`. The mesh automatically refines in regions of high-density gradients (i.e., the shock wave) up to a maximum of 3 refinement levels. It also coarsens the mesh where the gradients are low to maintain computational efficiency.

## Domain and Geometry

- **Geometry**: The domain is a 2D axisymmetric wedge, created with `blockMesh`, representing a slice of the flow field around the cone.
- **Initial Conditions**: The domain is filled with air at rest (`U = (0 0 0)`) and at a uniform pressure and temperature.

## Boundary Conditions

- **`movingWall`**: Represents the surface of the cone.
    - `U`: `movingWallVelocity` condition, which applies the velocity of the moving mesh points to the fluid.
    - `pointMotionUx`: A `uniformFixedValue` condition with a `table` provides the time-dependent velocity (160 m/s) for the points on this boundary.
- **`fixedWall`**: A stationary wall boundary.
- **`farField` & `farFieldMoving`**: Patches representing the far-field extent of the domain.
- **`front` and `back`**: `wedge` type boundaries to enforce axisymmetry.

## Numerical Schemes and Solution

- **Time Integration**: `ddtSchemes` uses the `Euler` method with a high-order `RK4SSP` (4th Order Runge-Kutta) integrator.
- **Flux Scheme**: `HLLC` (Harten-Lax-van Leer-Contact) is used, which is a robust and accurate choice for compressible flows with shocks.
- **Interpolation**: `vanLeer` limiters are used for reconstructing field variables, which helps to produce sharp, non-oscillatory shock profiles.
- **Solution Strategy**: The `fvSolution` file specifies a `PIMPLE` loop, a pressure-based algorithm for transient simulations, to handle the coupling between pressure and velocity. The mesh motion equation for `cellMotionUx` is solved using a `PCG` solver.

## Purpose and Learning Points

This case serves as an excellent tutorial for:
- Simulating problems with moving boundaries and resulting fluid-structure interaction (one-way, as the structure's motion is prescribed).
- Combining mesh motion with adaptive refinement (`movingAdaptiveFvMesh`).
- Capturing shock waves generated by high-speed objects.
- Using advanced numerical schemes (`HLLC`, `RK4SSP`, `vanLeer`) for high-fidelity compressible flow simulations.
